# Speaking Tips for Builders

Practical advice for developers who aren't natural presenters.

## The #1 Rule

**Tell a story, not a tutorial.** Nobody cares about your tech stack. They care about what happened to you while using it.

Bad: "So I set up MCP servers with three different tools and configured the CLAUDE.md file..."
Good: "I was mass-deleting test files and Claude nuked my entire src folder. Here's what I learned."

## Before Your Talk

- **Know your one takeaway.** If the audience remembers one thing, what is it?
- **Write your opening line.** "I was [emotion] when Claude Code [thing]." Practice saying it out loud once.
- **Don't prepare slides.** Unless you're doing a demo. Just talk.
- **Time yourself once.** 5 minutes is shorter than you think. Cut 30% of what you planned.

## During Your Talk

- **Start with the opening line.** Don't warm up. Don't introduce yourself first. Hit the line.
- **Look at people, not your screen.** If you're demoing, glance at the screen, then look back at the audience.
- **Pause instead of filling.** Silence is better than "um" or "basically."
- **If your demo breaks, just talk.** Describe what would have happened. The story matters more than the proof.
- **End with your takeaway.** Don't trail off. Land it.

## Common Developer Speaking Mistakes

1. **Starting with context nobody needs.** "So first let me explain what MCP is..." - Skip it. Your audience already knows.
2. **Showing too much code.** Nobody can read 50 lines on a projector. Show 5 lines max, or just talk about it.
3. **Hedging everything.** "I kind of think maybe this sort of works..." - Own your opinion. Be direct.
4. **Running over time.** Respect the timer. Stopping at exactly 5 minutes shows you prepared.
5. **Apologizing.** "Sorry this is a bad demo" - Don't. Just keep going.

## Table Topics Survival Guide

You just got called up with no preparation. Here's how to not panic:

1. **Repeat the question** to buy yourself 3 seconds of thinking time.
2. **Pick one specific moment or example.** Don't try to give a comprehensive answer.
3. **Use the structure: "So, [answer]. For example, [story]. That's why [takeaway]."**
4. **60 seconds is long enough.** You don't need to fill 2 minutes. A tight 60-second answer beats a rambling 2-minute one.
5. **It's okay to say "I don't know, but..."** and pivot to something you do know.

## Getting Better Over Time

- Speak at every event, even if just in table topics
- Watch your filler word count from the Ah Counter and try to reduce it
- Ask for feedback after your talks - from humans, not just the agent
- Record yourself practicing (even on your phone) and watch it back once
- Volunteer to go first. It gets easier after the first one.
